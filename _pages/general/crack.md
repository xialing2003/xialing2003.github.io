## Crack Opening

There are two criteria for crack opening. 

The first comes from the energy perspective. A crack would propagate if the accompanying release of potential energy is sufficient to provide the energy necessary for fracture. The potential energy consists of elastic strain energy with gravity or other work done to the rock. In the equation form, propagation occurs when $$G = G_c$$, where $G$ is the potential energy release per unit increase in crack length and $G_c$ is the critical value of $G$ required for propagation. $G_c$ is understood as the surface energy of the material, or equivalently, the work required to separate two planes of atoms. Actually, $G_c$ for rock exceeds the value of silicates by about 2 orders of magnitude, which may due to a grain-scale zone of microcracking surrounding the crack tip. 

Another criterion is based on the crack-tip stess intensity factor, which is more commonly used but less general. Equationally, the crack keeps propagating if $K = K_c$. $K$ is a measure of stress singularity at the crack tip and depends on the crack size and loading configuration. In the region close to the crack tip, the elastic stress is dominated by the term $K/\sqrt{r}$ and we call this region _K-dominant_ _region_. Real materials cannot withstand infinite stresses and instead deform inelastically near the crack tip. Provided that the region of inelastic deformation is small compared to the _K-dominant_ _region_, $K=K_c$ is a valid crack propagation criterion. 

When LEFM(linear elastic fracture mechanism) is applicable, $K$ and $G$ are related through $G = \pi K^2/2M$.
